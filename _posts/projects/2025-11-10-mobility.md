# Comparing Two Mobility Companies: From Raw Data to Business Recommendations



## The Challenge

I was given trip data from two mobility companies (Company A and Company B) operating in the same city. No schema, no documentation, no domain brief. The goal: understand how they operate, compare them, and estimate Company B’s fleet size—even through given data.

tl;dr: Click this link [(github link to the main notebook)](https://github.com/kim-datascience/personal_project/blob/main/mobility_data_analysis/operation_description.ipynb) to find a summary, plots, and codes.




[Visit my GitHub to see the codes and plots](https://github.com/kim-datascience/personal_project/blob/main/mobility_data_analysis)

## Starting from Scratch

The first step was making sense of the data. In **eda.ipynb**, I:

- **Inferred units** — Company A’s distance field had no label. I compared it to haversine distances from latitude/longitude coordinates and confirmed it was in miles.
- **Cleaned invalid records** — Dropped outliers (e.g., negative distances, implausible durations, etc seemingly wrong records).
- **Documented decisions** — Each filter is justified so the pipeline is reproducible and defensible.

## Operational Comparison

**operation_description.ipynb** turns the cleaned data into a structured operational view.

Observing the three peak clusters, by matching their coordinates with a map, I segmented the city into three areas—Downtown, University, and Residential—and tracked usage over time. The patterns were clear:

- **Company A**: Usage falls through fall and winter. Peak usage is afternoon, weekends, Downtown and Residential. Adding scooters did not help increasing rides; utilization rate dropped only (less effective operation).
- **Company B**: Usage rises in December. Peak usage is morning, weekdays, University. They appear to target university commuters, who have some commitment in usage regardless of weather, etc.

**Recommendations for Company A:**

1. **Target university commuters** — They keep riding in cold weather. Consider discounts or incentives for regular commuters.
2. **Right-size the fleet** — More scooters did not increase revenue. Only a waste.
3. **Dynamic pricing** — Usage is higher in warm weather, weekends, and Downtown; consider raising prices in those peak usage conditions.

## Estimating Fleet Size of Company B

Company B has no scooter IDs, so getting the exact fleet size is impossible. **estimate_num_scooter_company_b.ipynb** evaluates three approaches:

1. **Chain matching (B-only)** — Match trip end/start locations to infer scooters. Rejected: location resolution (~1 mile) is too coarse.
2. **Proportional scaling from A** — Use rides-per-scooter from A and scale. Rejected: rides are not proportional to fleet size (A’s data shows that).
3. **Assumed rides-per-scooter** — Use a plausible constant and divide total rides. Chosen: simple, transparent, and works with the data.

I report estimates under three assumptions (December mean, max, and min rides-per-scooter from A) to bound the result and show sensitivity.
